K-Pop Fandom Glossary: Bias, Stan, Comeback, and 30+ Terms

K-pop has its own language, and most of it isn't obvious from context. If you've ever read a stan tweet that contained five words you didn't know, this is the cheat sheet. Bookmark it. We've split the terms into categories so you can scan to what you need.

Bias and Fandom Terms

Bias

Your favorite member of a K-pop group. The foundational stan term. Full breakdown in our bias guide.

Ult / Ult Bias

Ultimate bias. Your #1 across all of K-pop, not just within one group. More on ults here.

Bias Wrecker

The member who keeps almost stealing your bias spot. Full explainer.

Stan

To be a devoted fan. "I stan ATEEZ" means you actively follow and support them. From the Eminem song "Stan," now stripped of negative connotations.

Multi-Stan

Someone who stans many groups. The default for most active K-pop fans, since algorithms surface new content constantly.

Solo Stan / OT-Stan

Solo stan: someone who stans only one specific member of a group, not the group itself. OT-stan: someone who stans the whole group equally (OT7 for a 7-member group).

Antifandom / Anti

Someone who actively dislikes a group or member. The opposite of stanning. Generally avoided in healthy fandom spaces.

Akgae

A toxic solo stan who actively dislikes the other members of their bias's group. From Korean "악개" (akgae). Distinct from a normal solo stan, who simply prefers one member.

Sasaeng

An obsessive fan who crosses into stalking, harassment, or invasive behavior. The community considers sasaengs harmful and most healthy fandoms condemn the behavior.

Fandom Name

The official collective name for a group's fans. ARMY (BTS), BLINK (BLACKPINK), Carat (SEVENTEEN), STAY (Stray Kids), Bunnies (NewJeans), MOA (TXT), MY (aespa), Engene (ENHYPEN).

Group Position Terms

Maknae

The youngest member of the group. Korean for "youngest." Often plays up a cute or mischievous role.

Leader

The designated spokesperson and decision-maker of the group. Usually but not always the oldest.

Visual

The member positioned as the group's visual centerpiece. A marketing-assigned role, not a fan vote.

Main Vocal / Lead Vocal

The strongest singers, ranked by their official title. Main is the top vocalist; lead handles supporting vocal duty.

Main Dancer / Lead Dancer

Same ranking idea applied to dance. Main carries the choreography; lead handles supporting dance positions.

Main Rapper / Lead Rapper

The rap line. Main rapper drops the hardest verses; lead rapper supports.

Rap Line / Vocal Line / Dance Line

The members of the group fulfilling each function. Rap line = all rappers. Vocal line = all vocalists. Dance line = the strongest dancers regardless of vocal/rap position.

Line Distribution

How many seconds of singing or rapping each member gets in a song. Calculated and posted by fans, often a source of fandom complaint when a bias gets too few lines.

Release and Activity Terms

Debut

A group's first official release and activity. Debuts are huge events — pre-debut content, debut showcase, and a debut album all build hype.

Comeback

Any new release after a break. Western fans say "new album"; K-pop says "comeback" even if the previous release was only three months ago.

Era

A single promotional cycle. An era includes the album, the music video, the styling, the choreography, and all the activities tied to one specific release. Fans refer to past eras like calendar markers.

Title Track

The lead single from an album. The song that gets the music video, music show performances, and chart promotion.

B-Side

Any album track that isn't a title track. Some B-sides are beloved deep cuts; fans frequently argue that certain B-sides should have been promoted instead.

Mini Album / EP

A short album of typically 4–7 tracks. Most K-pop releases are minis rather than full-lengths.

Repackage

A re-release of an existing album with one or two new tracks and updated packaging. Designed to extend an era and drive additional sales.

Sub-Unit

A smaller subgroup within a larger group. SEVENTEEN has three sub-units (hip-hop, vocal, performance); BSS is a separate three-member SEVENTEEN sub-unit. NCT has 127, Dream, and WayV.

Pre-Debut

Content released before the official debut — teaser MVs, intro videos, trainee performances. Pre-debut fans get bragging rights for stanning early.

Hiatus

A planned break from group activity, usually for member enlistment, individual schedules, or label reasons.

Soloist

An idol who performs alone, not in a group. Many group members also release solo material while staying in their group. IU is the most famous K-pop soloist.

OT(N)

"One True N" — where N is the number of members. OT7 means stanning all 7. Used by fans who refuse to bias-pick or who want to emphasize loyalty to the full group.

OT(N-1)

When a member leaves or is hated by a faction of fandom, you'll see "OT7 minus one" formulations like OT6. Often a sign of fandom drama.

Content and Event Terms

Fancam

A video that follows one member throughout an entire performance, either fan-shot or label-released. The most efficient way to bias-pick — see our bias-finding guide.

Lightstick

The official illuminated stick fans hold at concerts. Each group has a unique shape and color (ARMY Bomb, BLACKPINK Hammer, Carat Bong). Bluetooth-connected lightsticks sync with concert lighting.

Fanchant

The synchronized shouting of member names or specific lyrics by the crowd during a song. Most title tracks have official fanchants released in advance so fans can practice.

Photocard (PC)

A small printed card of an individual member, included in album versions. Fans collect, trade, and display them. Photocards are a massive secondary economy on their own.

Era Card

The version of a photocard tied to a specific era. Fans hunt for "all-era" sets of their bias.

Bubble / Weverse

Direct-message style apps where fans pay monthly for text-style updates from idols. Bubble is DearU's platform; Weverse is HYBE's. Both are essentially text-based parasocial connection.

Music Show

Weekly Korean TV programs (Music Bank, Show Champion, M Countdown, Inkigayo, The Show) where groups perform their title tracks. Winning a music show is a milestone.

Trophy / Win

Winning #1 on a music show. Groups often cry on the first trophy of a comeback because they're calculated as standing for fan effort, not just the song's quality.

Variety / VLive

Variety: Korean TV game and reality shows where idols guest-star. VLive: the original live-streaming platform for idols, now merged into Weverse. Both are gateways to bias decisions.

Comeback Stage / Goodbye Stage

The first and last performance of a comeback. Comeback stage launches the era; goodbye stage closes it.

Choreo / Choreography

The dance routine for a song. K-pop choreography is a defining art form of the genre, with full performances on music shows weekly.

Killing Part

The most memorable moment of a song — usually a vocal run, a rap verse, or a choreography highlight. Fans replay killing parts on TikTok endlessly.

Center

The member in the choreographic center during a key formation, usually during the chorus. Center is a marketing-significant position.

Tier List

A visual S/A/B/C/D ranking of members or songs. Read more on tier lists vs sorters.

Sorter

A pairwise-comparison tool that produces a ranking through head-to-head matchups. K-Pop Bias Battle is one example.

Generation (Gen)

One of five rough K-pop eras (1st gen through 5th gen). Full explainer here.

Less-Common But Useful Terms

Center Time

How much time a member spends in the visual center during a song's choreo. Tracked by fans the same way line distribution is.

Visual Hole

A member with stunning visuals. Affectionate term, no negative implication.

4D

A member whose personality is unpredictable or eccentric. "He's so 4D" means he's lovably weird.

Aegyo

Cute, baby-like behavior performed for fans or on variety. Most idols can do aegyo on demand. Some hate it but still get asked.

Selca

A selfie taken by an idol. From "self-camera." Posted to social media and treasured by fans.

Hyung / Noona / Unnie / Oppa

Korean honorifics. Hyung = older brother (male to male); noona = older sister (male to female); unnie = older sister (female to female); oppa = older brother (female to male). Used inside groups and by fans.

Daebak

Korean for "amazing" or "jackpot." Used as an interjection when something incredible happens.

Jjang

Korean for "the best." Often paired with a member's name as a hype phrase.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does OT mean in K-pop?

OT stands for "one true" followed by the number of members in a group. OT7 means stanning all 7 members equally. OT5 for a 5-member group, OT9 for a 9-member group. It's a way of saying you bias the whole group, not just one person.

What is a comeback in K-pop?

A comeback is when a K-pop group releases a new album, single, or EP after a break — even a short one. Western artists call this "releasing new music." Korean industry tradition treats every new release as a return to activity, so it's a "comeback."

What is a lightstick?

A lightstick is the official illuminated stick fans hold at concerts and fan meetings. Each group has its own unique lightstick shape and color. Bluetooth-connected lightsticks can sync with concert lighting cues for massive coordinated effects.

What does maknae mean?

Maknae means the youngest member of a K-pop group. It's a Korean word for the youngest in any group, family, or team. Members often play up cute or mischievous traits as the maknae role.

What is a fanchant?

A fanchant is the synchronized shouting of member names or specific lyrics during a song, performed by the audience at live shows. Most title tracks have official fanchants released by the label so fans can practice in advance.
