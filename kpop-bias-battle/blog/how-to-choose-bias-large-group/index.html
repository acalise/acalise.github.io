How to Pick a Bias in a Group With 18+ Members

If you've ever opened a SEVENTEEN performance video and felt actual panic at 13 faces moving in synchronized perfection — or scrolled an NCT lineup poster and just closed the tab — this article is for you. Picking a bias in a 5-member group is hard. Picking one in a 13, 20, or 24-member group is a different sport. Here's how to do it without crying.

Why Large Groups Are So Hard

Your brain has a limit. Most cognitive psychology suggests humans can comfortably hold 5–9 items in working memory at once. Beyond that, things start dropping out. So when you look at a SEVENTEEN group photo, you're literally not seeing all 13 members — your attention is rotating through subsets without you noticing.

The fix is to stop trying to process the full group at once. Break it into smaller pieces and pick within those. The same approach our general bias-finding guide uses, but applied with more discipline.

Step 1: Learn the Sub-Units First

Almost every large K-pop group is sub-divided. SEVENTEEN has three official sub-units (hip-hop, vocal, performance). NCT splits into 127, Dream, WayV, and various rotating projects. tripleS has multiple acronymed sub-units across its 24 members. Use those divisions as your first filter.

SEVENTEEN's Three Units

The hip-hop unit (S.Coups, Wonwoo, Mingyu, Vernon) handles rap and harder verses. The vocal unit (Jeonghan, Joshua, Woozi, DK, Seungkwan) carries the melodic backbone. The performance unit (Hoshi, Jun, The8, Dino) leads choreography and stage execution. Watch one unit's solo content before tackling all 13 members together.

NCT's Sub-Groups

NCT 127 is the Seoul-based core, NCT Dream is the younger group originally built around a graduation concept (now permanent), WayV is the China-based group. Each has its own sound and aesthetic. Almost every NCT fan starts by biasing within one sub-group, not across the whole "NCT" identity.

tripleS's Sub-Units

tripleS has 24 members organized into multiple acronymed sub-units (Acid Angel from Asia, +(KR)ystal Eyes, LOVElution, EVOLution, etc.) that fans literally vote into existence using the Cosmo app. The whole group only performs together occasionally. Most tripleS fans pick a bias by sub-unit first.

Step 2: Filter by Position

Once you've picked a unit, narrow further by position. In K-pop, every member has an official role:

  • Main / lead vocal: The strongest singers
  • Main / lead rapper: The rap line
  • Main / lead dancer: The performance core
  • Visual: Often a marketing role, the member positioned as the visual centerpiece
  • Maknae: The youngest, often given specific charm roles
  • Leader: The spokesperson and decision-maker

If you know you like vocal-heavy K-pop, prioritize the vocal line. If you live for rap verses, focus on the rappers. Your existing taste should narrow the field by at least 50% before you even start comparing.

Step 3: Watch Sub-Unit Solo Content

Most large groups have content where members are clearly separated:

  • SEVENTEEN's "Going Seventeen" often splits the group into teams or pairs
  • NCT's "NCT Daily" rotates which members appear week to week
  • tripleS publishes content per sub-unit
  • Sub-unit comebacks (BSS for SEVENTEEN, 127 vs Dream for NCT) are gifts for narrowing

Pick one sub-unit's content and watch a few episodes. You'll start to identify three or four members who click for you. That's your shortlist.

Step 4: Run the Sorter on Your Shortlist

Once you've narrowed to four or five candidates, this is where a sorter does its best work. Pairwise comparison settles ties your brain can't. Two SEVENTEEN performance unit members might both feel like 9/10 picks — the sorter forces one to be 9.2 and the other to be 9.1, and that's enough to settle the bias question.

You don't have to run the sorter on all 13 or 20+ members. Just feed it your shortlist. The point isn't comprehensiveness — it's resolution at the top. Read more on the methodology in our sorter vs tier list explainer.

Step 5: Accept the "Bias Line" Concept

In groups this size, even after all the filtering, you'll often end up with three to five members who feel like co-biases. That's fine. The fandom calls this a "bias line" — a top tier rather than a single pick. SEVENTEEN fans often have a "carat line" of three to five favorites and rotate which one they call "bias" depending on the comeback.

Resisting this and forcing a single name actually makes the experience worse. Embrace the bias line. Update your sorter every few months. Let your top 3 shuffle.

Specific Approach Tips by Group

For SEVENTEEN (13 members)

Watch "Going Seventeen" — it's possibly the best K-pop variety content out there. After 3–5 episodes you'll have firm opinions on at least 6 members. From there, run a sorter on those 6.

For NCT (20+ members across units)

Don't try to bias-pick across all of NCT. Pick one sub-group. NCT Dream is the easiest entry point — younger members, more cohesive group dynamic, less complex history. NCT 127 has the strongest discography. WayV has the strongest dance line.

For tripleS (24 members)

Start with one acronymed sub-unit's MVs. There are too many members to absorb cold. Use the Cosmo app to actually engage with the fan-voting mechanic, which forces you to learn members through participation.

For ZB1 (9 members) and similar

Slightly smaller groups (9–12 members) are easier — you don't need sub-units to navigate, just patience. Watch one full performance, identify your top three, then run a sorter on those three.

What Makes a Bias in a Large Group Different

  • Less line time means more deliberate appreciation. Your bias in SEVENTEEN gets less screen time per song than your bias in NewJeans. Stanning becomes about following individual moments more than full songs.
  • Sub-unit comebacks become high-stakes events. When your bias is in the smaller sub-unit comeback, those releases hit different.
  • Bias wreckers happen constantly. Statistically, with 13–24 members rotating through content, your bias and wrecker positions are going to shift more than in a 5-member group. Plan for it.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do you pick a bias in SEVENTEEN?

Start with their sub-units. SEVENTEEN is divided into hip-hop, vocal, and performance units. Pick the unit that sounds most like the music you already love, then narrow within that unit.

How do you pick a bias in NCT?

NCT has multiple sub-groups (127, Dream, WayV, NCT U). Pick one sub-group first — most fans bias-pick within a single sub-unit rather than across all of NCT at once. Then narrow inside that unit.

Is it okay to have multiple biases in a large group?

Yes. In groups of 18 or more members, most fans treat the top three to five members as a "bias line" rather than picking one. Tracking your top tier and your runners-up is more useful than forcing a single name.

How long does it take to bias-pick in a 20-member group?

If you watch one sub-unit at a time, you can usually settle on a bias within two to three weeks of casual exposure. Using a sorter, the math itself takes about 10 to 15 minutes for groups that size.

Why are some K-pop groups so big?

Large groups can run multiple sub-units in parallel, target different demographics, and survive member enlistments or departures more easily. The trade-off is overwhelming the audience, which is why most 5th gen groups have shrunk to 4 to 7 members.
